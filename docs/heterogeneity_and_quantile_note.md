# Heterogeneity and Quantile Checks: Evidence Against Profit Shifting

The headline OLS coefficient (−68.2 on Tariff × Post2018, p < 0.001) is large and survives the standard robustness battery (alternative FEs, balanced panel, placebo). But when the effect is decomposed by firm characteristics and by the ETR distribution, the pattern does **not** match what a profit-shifting mechanism would predict. Documenting that here so the interpretation in the paper is honest.

## 1. Heterogeneity: who responds?

Under a profit-shifting story, the tariff response should be concentrated in firms that actually have the ability to shift:

1. Firms with pre-existing foreign operations (subsidiaries, foreign IP, transfer-pricing infrastructure)
2. Firms with high R&D intensity (IP-based transfer pricing is the main channel)
3. Large multinationals (scale to sustain international tax planning)

All three predictions fail.

| Specification | Coef | SE | p | N |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| H1: Base (low-FPS firms) | −56.9 | 15.2 | 0.001 | 3,308 |
| H1: + High-FPS interaction | −6.2 | 13.0 | 0.637 | 3,308 |
| H1: Total effect for high-FPS firms | −63.1 | — | — | 3,308 |
| H2: Split sample — high foreign profit share only | −46.0 | 26.0 | 0.092 | 2,060 |
| H3: Split sample — low foreign profit share only | −61.8 | 29.2 | 0.046 | 1,248 |
| H4: Continuous pre-FPS interaction | +0.8 | 0.8 | 0.329 | 3,308 |
| H5: Base (low-R&D firms) | −111.0 | 26.1 | <0.001 | 4,129 |
| H5: + High-R&D interaction | +30.5 | 15.9 | 0.068 | 4,129 |
| H6: Base (small firms) | −67.0 | 13.4 | <0.001 | 4,150 |
| H6: + Large-firm interaction | −0.6 | 16.1 | 0.968 | 4,150 |

Read-out:
- **Foreign operations.** Firms *without* foreign operations respond at least as strongly as firms with them (−61.8 vs −46.0 in split samples; interaction term is essentially zero). Firms that cannot shift profits abroad cannot be driving a profit-shifting result.
- **R&D intensity.** The response is concentrated in *low*-R&D firms (−111 in the base, with the high-R&D interaction pulling the coefficient back toward zero at +30.5). The transfer-pricing-on-IP channel predicts the opposite sign.
- **Firm size.** Large firms do not respond differently from small firms. Large multinationals have the tax-planning resources; small firms do not.

Every channel runs against the profit-shifting prediction.

## 2. Quantile regression: where in the ETR distribution is the effect?

A behavioral shift in tax planning should move a meaningful mass of firms' ETRs, not just the tails. Quantile fixed-effects regression with cluster-bootstrapped standard errors:

| Quantile | Coef | Boot SE | 95% CI |
|---|---|---|---|
| τ = 0.25 | −11.5 | 4.8 | [−18.2, +1.1] |
| τ = 0.50 (median) | −0.4 | 1.1 | [−2.5, +1.7] |
| τ = 0.75 | −0.6 | 5.2 | [−12.5, +7.0] |
| τ = 0.50 on [0, 60] trimmed ETR | −0.6 | 0.9 | [−2.4, +1.2] |

At the median firm, the effect is zero. The OLS coefficient of −68 is driven by the tails of the ETR distribution, not by a broad-based decline in effective tax rates.

## 3. Interpretation

Taken together, the results are inconsistent with a profit-shifting mechanism. A more plausible reading is **mechanical margin compression in the ETR denominator**:

- Section 301 tariffs raise input costs for goods-producing firms.
- Pre-tax income compresses; some firms land near zero or negative income.
- ETR = taxes / pre-tax income becomes unstable and takes large values (positive or negative) when the denominator shrinks.
- OLS picks up this tail behavior, producing a large average coefficient even though most firms' ETRs do not move.

This story fits the evidence: (a) the median firm shows no change, (b) the result concentrates in firms that cannot shift (low-FPS, low-R&D, smaller), and (c) the magnitude is sensitive to how aggressively ETR outliers are trimmed (falls from −68 to −24 to −8 as winsorization tightens).

## 4. Implications for the paper

- The OLS specification is not evidence of profit shifting. Framing the paper around profit shifting as the causal mechanism is no longer defensible.
- A cleaner framing: "Tariff-exposed firms exhibit large OLS reductions in effective tax rates, but the response fails structural tests for profit shifting (heterogeneity runs the wrong way; median effect is zero). The reduction is better interpreted as mechanical compression of the ETR denominator when pre-tax income is squeezed by tariff-driven cost increases."
- Future work: decompose the ETR response into numerator (cash taxes) vs denominator (pre-tax income) movements. If the denominator is doing the work, this is a margin story, not a tax-planning story.
